## 1. Company Overview: Intuitive Machines (NASDAQ: LUNR)

Intuitive Machines is a Houston-based space exploration and infrastructure company that went public via SPAC in February 2023. The company operates across four business units: Lunar Access Services, Orbital Services, Lunar Data Services, and Space Products & Infrastructure. Its core product is the Nova-C class lunar lander, built under NASA’s Commercial Lunar Payload Services (CLPS) program to deliver payloads to the Moon.

LUNR has four manifested lunar missions under CLPS (IM-1 through IM-4) and has been expanding aggressively beyond landing services into communications infrastructure, orbital vehicles, and defense contracts.

**Sources to verify:** Intuitive Machines investor page (investors.intuitivemachines.com), SEC filings, company website (intuitivemachines.com).

## 2. Mission History: IM-1 and IM-2

### IM-1 — Odysseus (February 2024)

Odysseus launched on a SpaceX Falcon 9 on February 15, 2024 and landed near Malapert A crater in the lunar south pole region on February 22. It was the first American spacecraft to soft-land on the Moon since Apollo 17 in 1972, and the first commercial lander to reach the lunar surface.

However, the landing was rough. The spacecraft’s primary laser rangefinders were inoperable due to a safety switch that wasn’t activated during pre-launch prep. Engineers attempted to reroute navigation through NASA’s Navigation Doppler Lidar payload, but a data flag was missed in the software, meaning Odysseus ultimately landed using only its inertial measurement unit and optical navigation. One or more landing legs broke on contact, and the lander tipped to a 30-degree angle.

Despite this, payload data delivery was solid. Intuitive Machines reported that Odysseus transmitted over 350 megabytes of science and engineering data from all payloads before entering standby mode on February 29. NASA confirmed data was received from all five active payloads (the sixth was a passive laser reflector). NASA’s CLPS project scientist Sue Lederer stated that “every payload has met some level of their objective.” The lander operated for approximately 7 days, exceeding the 144-hour surface operations objective. Intuitive Machines CEO Steve Altemus called it “an unqualified success.”

Odysseus did not wake up after lunar night. The mission was declared over on March 23, 2024.

**Sources:** Intuitive Machines IM-1 mission page (intuitivemachines.com/im-1), Intuitive Machines press release Feb 29, 2024 (investors.intuitivemachines.com), SpaceNews article “Intuitive Machines and NASA call IM-1 lunar lander a success” (spacenews.com, Feb 29, 2024), Space.com “Intuitive Machines’ private Odysseus moon lander has permanently faded” (space.com, March 25, 2024), NASA NSSDCA page for IM-1 (nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov), Wikipedia IM-1 entry.

### IM-2 — Athena (March 2025)

Athena launched February 27, 2025 and reached the lunar surface on March 6, 2025 at Mons Mouton near the south pole — the southernmost lunar landing ever achieved. The lander touched down approximately 250 meters from its intended site, inside a crater.

The same category of failure recurred: the altimeter failed during landing, leaving the onboard computer without accurate altitude readings. Athena struck a plateau, tipped over, and skidded across the surface, rolling once or twice before settling inside the crater. CEO Altemus compared it to “a baseball player sliding into a base.” Regolith coated the solar panels, and the lander’s batteries depleted within approximately 13 hours of landing.

Payload results were significantly worse than IM-1. Intuitive Machines collected approximately 250 megabytes of data for NASA and completed some instrument checkouts before power ran out. The TRIDENT drill was extended but never operated. The Micro-Nova hopper and rovers could not be deployed. Nokia’s 4G/LTE technology demonstration only completed partial objectives. Lunar Outpost confirmed their MAPP rover survived the landing but could not be deployed due to the lander’s orientation.

NASA’s tone was notably more restrained than after IM-1, framing the results as learning opportunities rather than successes.

**Sources:** Intuitive Machines IM-2 mission page (intuitivemachines.com/im-2), Intuitive Machines press release March 7, 2025 (investors.intuitivemachines.com), NASA press release “NASA Receives Some Data Before Intuitive Machines Ends Lunar Mission” (nasa.gov, March 7, 2025), Spaceflight Now “Intuitive Machines’ IM-2 Moon mission ends with lander on its side” (spaceflightnow.com, March 7, 2025), Wikipedia IM-2 entry.

## 3. Altimeter Fix and IM-3 Plans

During Intuitive Machines’ Q1 2025 earnings call on May 14, 2025, CEO Steve Altemus detailed three root causes for IM-2’s landing failure:

1. Signal noise and distortion from the laser altimeters that prevented accurate altitude readings during final descent.
1. Low sun angles at the south pole creating long shadows that challenged the landing system’s precision.
1. Craters appearing differently at lower altitudes than in reference images from NASA’s Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter, affecting optical navigation.

For IM-3, the company is implementing: dissimilar and redundant altimeters with more rigorous flight-like testing, a lighting-independent sensor for surface velocity measurements, an expanded onboard terrain crater database, and machine learning improvements using imagery collected during IM-2. Altemus said this will result in a “slight cost increase” but no schedule impact.

Notably, IM-3 is headed to Reiner Gamma near the lunar equator — a much easier landing environment than the south pole. IM-4 will return to the south pole, which is where the real test of these fixes matters for Artemis relevance.

**Sources:** SpaceNews “Altimeter problems, lighting challenges caused IM-2 lunar lander to fall on its side” (spacenews.com, May 13, 2025), Spaceflight Now “Intuitive Machines’ CEO points to issues that prevented upright touchdown” (spaceflightnow.com, May 14, 2025), SpacePolicyOnline “IM-2’s Imperfect Landing Due to Altimeter Interference” (spacepolicyonline.com).

## 4. LUNR’s Broader Pipeline and Contracts

Beyond landing missions, LUNR has built a substantial contract backlog:

- **Near Space Network Services (NSNS):** A $4.82 billion contract with NASA for communications infrastructure, awarded through a joint venture (Space Networks Solutions LLC) led by Intuitive Machines with KBR.
- **Lunar Terrain Vehicle (LTV):** LUNR is competing for a ~$4.6 billion NASA contract to build a commercially provided rover for Artemis crewed missions. Final proposals are due in late July 2025, with an award expected in November 2025. Competitors are Lunar Outpost (Eagle rover) and Venturi Astrolab (FLEX rover). LUNR’s bid was reportedly the lowest at $1.692 billion vs. $1.727B (Lunar Outpost) and $1.928B (Astrolab). Lunar Outpost lost its principal partner Lockheed Martin in September 2024, which may weaken their position.
- **Lanteris Acquisition:** Completed in January 2026 for approximately $800 million, creating combined revenue exceeding $850 million with positive adjusted EBITDA and $920 million in backlog.
- **AFRL Contract:** $8.2 million contract extension for in-space nuclear power technology (JETSON project).
- **Lunar Data Network / Khonstellation:** Building a constellation of data relay satellites, with the first satellite (Khon2) planned for launch on IM-3.

**Sources:** Intuitive Machines investor press releases (investors.intuitivemachines.com), NASA NSNS contract announcement, SpaceNews LTV coverage, Intuitive Machines Q1 2025 earnings call transcript.

## 5. Competitive Landscape

### Firefly Aerospace — Blue Ghost Mission 1 (March 2025)

Firefly’s Blue Ghost lander launched January 15, 2025 and successfully landed upright at Mare Crisium on March 2 — just four days before IM-2’s troubled landing. It was the first fully successful commercial Moon landing ever.

Blue Ghost operated for over 14 days (346 hours) on the lunar surface. All 10 NASA payloads successfully operated and collected science data. The lander transmitted over 119 GB of data, including 51 GB of science and technology data, significantly surpassing mission requirements. NASA’s Joel Kearns called Blue Ghost 1 “an existence proof of how we wanted Commercial Lunar Payload Services to work.” The mission was awarded the 2025 SpaceNews Icon Award for Commercial Space Achievement.

Firefly has been awarded five CLPS task orders supporting four missions through 2029. Blue Ghost Mission 2 is already fully booked.

Firefly IPO’d on August 7, 2025 under the ticker FLY on the Nasdaq, pricing at $45/share and raising $868 million — the largest space and defense technology IPO ever. Shares opened at $70 and closed at $60.35 on day one. As of February 27, 2026, FLY is trading around $21.53, with a 52-week range of $16 to $73.80. Analyst consensus is Buy with an average 12-month price target of $38.29. Note: shareholder class action lawsuits have been filed following the post-IPO decline.

## 6. SpaceX IPO and Sector Catalyst

SpaceX merged with xAI (which had previously acquired X/Twitter in March 2025) on February 2, 2026. The deal valued SpaceX at $1 trillion and xAI at $250 billion, creating the world’s most valuable private company at $1.25 trillion. The merger was structured as an all-stock acquisition, with each xAI share converting to 0.1433 shares of SpaceX.

Musk plans to IPO the combined entity in mid-2026 (reportedly targeting July) at a valuation of up to $1.5 trillion. SpaceX reportedly generated $15 billion in revenue and approximately $8 billion in profit in 2025. xAI generated roughly $250 million in revenue over six months while losing $2.5 billion. At $1.5T, the combined entity would trade at approximately 94x trailing sales and 500x trailing earnings.

A SpaceX IPO would likely boost space sector stocks (including LUNR and FLY) through increased institutional attention, capital inflows, and valuation re-rating. However, if the market rejects the valuation, it could drag the sector down. Some analysts view the merger as primarily a bailout for cash-burning xAI using SpaceX’s profitable business.

## 7. Space ETFs — Hedging Across the Sector

For investors who want space exposure without single-stock risk, three primary ETFs are available, plus one with unique SpaceX access:

### UFO — Procure Space ETF (NASDAQ: UFO)

- **Expense ratio:** 0.94%
- **AUM:** ~$112 million
- **Focus:** The only self-described pure-play space ETF in the US. Tracks the S-Network Space Index. Holdings are companies with direct, substantial revenue from space operations.
- **Top holdings:** EchoStar (SATS), Viasat (VSAT), Rocket Lab (RKLB), with 40+ total stocks.
- **Performance:** 44.6% YTD gains as of late 2025 reporting.
- **Consideration:** Most direct space exposure but smallest AUM and highest expense ratio. Lower liquidity than ARKX.

### ARKX — ARK Space & Defense Innovation ETF (BATS: ARKX)

- **Expense ratio:** 0.75%
- **AUM:** ~$485 million (largest space ETF)
- **Focus:** Actively managed by Cathie Wood’s ARK Invest. Broader interpretation of “space” — includes defense, eVTOLs, autonomous tech, and companies with indirect space ties.
- **Top holdings:** L3Harris Technologies (LHX), Teradyne (TER), Kratos Defense (KTOS), Rocket Lab (RKLB).
- **Performance:** 31.8% YTD as of late 2025 reporting.
- **Consideration:** Most liquid option (avg 359K shares/day trading volume). Broader exposure means less pure space concentration — some holdings (historically including Deere, Netflix, Amazon) have tenuous space connections.

### ROKT — SPDR S&P Kensho Final Frontiers ETF (NYSEARCA: ROKT)

- **Expense ratio:** 0.45% (cheapest)
- **Focus:** AI-selected companies in both aerospace exploration and deep-sea exploration (“final frontiers”). Uses the S&P Kensho Final Frontiers Index.
- **Consideration:** Best for cost-conscious long-term holders. Dual space/ocean exposure is unique but dilutes pure space thesis.

### XOVR — (for SpaceX pre-IPO exposure)

- **Unique feature:** Holds approximately 10-11% of assets in SpaceX as a private equity position, giving retail investors rare indirect SpaceX access before the July 2026 IPO.
- **Consideration:** If your thesis partly depends on the SpaceX IPO lifting the sector, this is the only ETF offering direct SpaceX ownership right now.

## 8. Key Risks and Open Questions

- **LUNR landing execution:** Two consecutive missions with the same root cause failure (altimeter). Fixes are planned but unproven. IM-3’s equatorial landing site won’t test south pole performance.
- **LTV contract uncertainty:** Award expected November 2025. LUNR has the lowest bid and stable partners, but landing track record is a liability. Lunar Outpost lost Lockheed Martin but may find a replacement.
- **Sector valuation:** Space stocks are narrative-driven and pre-profitability. Both LUNR and FLY are trading well below their highs. The sector could re-rate upward on a SpaceX IPO or downward if that IPO disappoints.
- **SpaceX IPO composition:** Investors buying SpaceX will also be buying money-losing xAI and X/Twitter. The $1.5T target valuation implies extreme multiples. Some analysts view the xAI merger as a bailout rather than strategic synergy.
- **ETF expense ratios:** Space ETFs charge 0.45-0.94%, significantly higher than broad market index funds. Justified only if the sector outperforms.
- **Regulatory/political risk:** NASA budgets, Artemis program timelines, and defense spending priorities could shift. Trump’s executive order targets a 2028 Moon landing, but program execution depends on sustained funding.
